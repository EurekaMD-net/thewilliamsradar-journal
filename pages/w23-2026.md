## The Ticker of the Week — Deep Dive

> 🟡 ANALYST COMMENTARY — Editorial interpretation. Not model output.

### CHTR · Charter Communications, Inc.

**Why CHTR this week:** Charter Communications enters W23 at p0% — printing at its absolute 52-week low, the lowest price percentile reading in the entire 254-ticker universe this week. Of all the names the model covers across 13 sectors, Charter has the most price-compressed setup heading into W24. When the second-largest cable operator in the United States — 32 million passings, $54B in annual revenue — lands at a multi-year floor with fresh AO/AC deterioration and a new S1 signal, the system demands a look.

**The Business.** Charter Communications is the operator of the Spectrum brand, serving approximately 32 million homes and businesses across 41 states. Its core product suite spans broadband internet (the primary growth engine), video (a declining but still cash-generative legacy segment), and mobile (Spectrum Mobile, an MVNO on Verizon's network that has become one of the fastest-growing wireless providers in the country by lines added). Charter generates roughly $14B in annual EBITDA on $54B in revenue, making it one of the largest cash flow machines in US media infrastructure. The company does not pay a dividend — it converts cash flow directly into share buybacks, having repurchased over 40% of its diluted share count over the past decade.

**Why depressed.** Charter's stock is down approximately 60% from its 2021 peak of ~$825, and the decline has been grinding and persistent. Three structural forces explain it: (1) **Fiber overbuilding** — national fiber providers (AT&T, Lumen, BEAD-funded municipal builds) are passing an increasing percentage of Charter's existing cable footprint. The company estimates 55–60% of its serviceable homes will face fiber competition by 2026, compared to roughly 30% in 2022. Each new fiber overbuilder reduces Charter's pricing power and subscriber retention in that market. (2) **Cord-cutting acceleration** — video subscribers have declined from ~16M in 2018 to under 10M today. While video is a low-margin, high-churn product, the decline accelerates CPE costs and reduces the bundle stickiness that historically anchored broadband retention. (3) **Capital intensity** — Charter's network evolution program (NEX), designed to upgrade its HFC infrastructure to DOCSIS 3.1 and eventually a converged cable access platform, requires sustained elevated capex. Management has guided for $12B+ in annual capex through 2026, compressing free cash flow in the near term and creating a perception among investors that Charter is fighting a defensive infrastructure war on an unfavorable timeline. None of these trends are fatal — cable infrastructure remains extraordinarily durable, Charter's broadband ARPU continues to grow, and mobile is a genuine new revenue vertical — but together, they explain why institutional holders have been reducing exposure for three years.

**The Setup.** At current price levels, CHTR trades at approximately 6–7× EBITDA and roughly 10–12× forward free cash flow — multiples at the low end of its historical range and at a discount to peers like Comcast (CMCSA, also in the pre-radar at p5%). AO is deeply negative and AC has not yet crossed — the system is flagging deterioration, not entry. The S2 trigger we are watching for W24 is the AC inflection: when the shorter oscillator crosses toward green while AO remains below zero, that sets the S2 confirmation. With the name at p0%, the fundamental risk/reward for patient capital is skewed. A mean-reversion toward the mid-range of its 5-year trading band implies 40–70% upside from current levels — not because the overbuilding thesis resolves immediately, but because the current price is pricing in outcomes worse than even the most pessimistic fiber buildout scenarios. The signal tells you when the momentum structure confirms it. We are not there yet. We are watching.

**Risk factors.** The primary risk is not that fiber overbuilding accelerates beyond projections — it is that Charter's subscriber losses in overbuilt markets exceed management's guided churn rates, forcing a prolonged period of sub-EBITDA capex with no cash flow optionality for buybacks. A secondary risk is the MVNO structure of Spectrum Mobile: Charter rents capacity from Verizon, which creates a ceiling on mobile margins. If Charter needs to build its own wireless infrastructure to remain competitive, that capex requirement fundamentally changes the FCF story. A third risk is interest rate sensitivity — Charter carries ~$100B in long-term debt, and refinancing risk at elevated rates compresses equity value at the margin. These are known, priced-in risks. They explain p0%. They do not necessarily justify it.

**The patient investor case.** Charter's core asset — a licensed, embedded, high-bandwidth broadband infrastructure serving 32 million homes — cannot be easily replicated or replaced. Fiber builds take years and billions; even the most aggressive overbuilders are not fully competitive in the majority of Charter's footprint today. Broadband internet is not discretionary. ARPU continues to grow. Mobile adds are accelerating. The buyback machine, while paused during peak capex, is structurally intact. At p0%, the market is pricing Charter like a melting ice cube. The system is designed to find exactly these setups. We watch for the AC cross.
